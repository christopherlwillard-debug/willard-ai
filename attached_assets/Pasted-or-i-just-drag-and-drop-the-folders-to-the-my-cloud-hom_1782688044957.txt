Honestly, I like that approach even more.

You don't necessarily need a complicated Import Engine if your workflow is always going to be:

Download photos (Google Photos, iCloud, SD card, etc.).
Drag and drop them into a folder on your NAS.
Your app notices the new folder.
Your app processes it.
Immich indexes it.

That simplifies the entire system.

Your workflow could look like this
Google Photos ZIP
        │
Extract
        │
Drag folder to:
Z:\Photos\Incoming
        │
        ▼
Replit detects new folder
        │
        ├── Read EXIF
        ├── Detect duplicates
        ├── Rename (optional)
        ├── Organize into final folders
        ├── Generate metadata
        └── Trigger Immich rescan

No upload interface. No browser file picker. No waiting for uploads.

Even better: an "Incoming" folder

Instead of dropping files anywhere, dedicate one folder, for example:

Photos/
├── Incoming/
├── Personal/
├── Manufactured Homes/
├── Asphalt/
└── Archive/

Your app continuously watches Incoming.

When a new folder appears:

It validates the files.
Detects duplicates.
Organizes them.
Moves them to their final location.
Triggers an Immich rescan.
Marks the import as complete.

That gives you a very clean workflow.

One feature I'd definitely add

An Import Queue in your app.

Instead of processing everything silently, show something like:

Import Queue

📁 Google Photos 2026
Status: Processing...

✔ 1,842 photos imported
✔ 27 videos imported
⚠ 3 duplicates skipped
✔ Immich scan triggered

Completed

Now you always know what happened without digging through logs.

I think this is the simplest architecture
NAS = permanent storage.
Replit app = watches and organizes.
Immich = AI indexing and search.

That's fewer moving parts, easier to troubleshoot, and it still gives you all the AI features you're after.

For your use case, I'd actually prefer this over building a full upload/import interface. It keeps the workflow fast and lets your app focus on organizing and managing media rather than transferring files.
